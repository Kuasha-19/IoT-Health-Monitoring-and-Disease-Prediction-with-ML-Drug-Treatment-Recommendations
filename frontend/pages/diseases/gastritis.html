Gastritis: Pathophysiology of the Gastric Mucosa

Definition and Clinical Presentation

Gastritis is defined as the histological identification of inflammation within the gastric mucosa. It is a process occurring when the protective mucosal barrier is compromised, allowing hydrochloric acid and pepsin to cause autodigestion of the stomach lining. Clinical manifestations often include dyspepsia, postprandial fullness, and epigastric pain. In more severe cases, patients may present with nausea, vomiting, or even gastrointestinal bleeding manifesting as black tarry stools.

Etiology and Classification

The condition is broadly classified into acute and chronic forms. Acute gastritis often results from erosive agents like NSAIDs, alcohol, or severe stress (stress gastritis). Chronic gastritis is frequently secondary to persistent infection by Helicobacter pylori, which induces a chronic inflammatory response. Other causes include bile reflux, autoimmune disorders, and certain viral infections.

How Common Is Gastritis?

Gastritis affects millions of people worldwide. H. pylori infection is the leading cause, with prevalence exceeding 50% in some developing countries. In the United States, about 8 million outpatient visits each year are related to gastritis or dyspepsia. While acute gastritis often resolves quickly, chronic gastritis can persist for years and increases the risk of peptic ulcers and gastric cancer, especially when associated with H. pylori.

Grading the Severity of Gastritis

Histologically, gastritis is graded based on the degree of inflammation and glandular loss. The updated Sydney System is commonly used, which assesses three features:

  • Inflammation: Mild, moderate, or marked infiltration of lymphocytes and plasma cells.
  • Activity: Presence of neutrophil granulocytes indicating active inflammation.
  • Atrophy: Loss of glandular tissue, graded as none, mild, moderate, or severe.
  • Intestinal metaplasia: Replacement of gastric epithelium by intestinal-type cells, a precancerous change.

Risk Factors for Gastritis

Several factors increase the likelihood of developing gastritis. Some are within our control; others are not.

Infectious causes

  • Helicobacter pylori infection – the most common cause worldwide.
  • Other bacterial or viral infections (rare in immunocompetent individuals).

Chemical/irritant exposure

  • Regular use of NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen, aspirin).
  • Heavy alcohol consumption.
  • Bile reflux after gastric surgery.
  • Cocaine use (can cause severe gastritis).

Host-related factors

  • Autoimmune conditions (autoimmune gastritis).
  • Severe physiological stress (burns, trauma, critical illness).
  • Advanced age – gastric mucosa becomes more vulnerable.
  • Pernicious anemia (associated with autoimmune gastritis).

Reducing the Risk of Gastritis

While not all cases can be prevented, many lifestyle measures help protect the stomach lining.

  • Use NSAIDs cautiously. Take with food or consider alternative pain relievers like acetaminophen.
  • Limit alcohol. Excessive drinking directly irritates the gastric mucosa.
  • Avoid smoking. Tobacco weakens the stomach's protective barrier.
  • Manage stress. Although stress alone doesn't cause chronic gastritis, it can worsen symptoms.
  • Practice good hygiene. Wash hands regularly to reduce H. pylori transmission.
  • If you have H. pylori, complete the full course of antibiotics. This eradicates the infection and prevents recurrence.

Recent Advances in Understanding Gastritis

Gastroenterology research continues to refine our knowledge of gastritis and its long‑term consequences.

H. pylori eradication regimens

Quadruple therapy and rifabutin‑based regimens are now used in areas with high clarithromycin resistance. Studies show that successful eradication reduces gastric cancer risk by about 50% in high‑risk populations.

Biomarkers for gastric atrophy

Serum pepsinogen I/II ratios and gastrin‑17 levels are increasingly used to non‑invasively screen for atrophic gastritis and stratify cancer risk, especially in Asia and parts of Europe.

Gut microbiome and gastritis

Emerging evidence suggests that the composition of the gastric microbiota (beyond H. pylori) may influence the progression to atrophy and metaplasia. Probiotics are being studied as adjuncts to standard therapy.

When to seek further evaluation: Patients with persistent dyspepsia, unintended weight loss, or signs of gastrointestinal bleeding (vomiting blood, black stools) should undergo upper endoscopy with biopsies to confirm the diagnosis and rule out complications.

Clinical Types

  • Erosive Gastritis: Characterized by mucosal surface depletion and shallow ulcerations often caused by chemical irritants like NSAIDs or alcohol. Endoscopy reveals petechiae and erosions.
  • Non-erosive Gastritis: Primarily refers to histological changes induced by chronic H. pylori infection, often without visible mucosal breaks.
  • Atrophic Gastritis: Chronic inflammation leading to the loss of glandular cells and intrinsic factor secretion. It raises the risk of gastric adenocarcinoma.
  • Autoimmune Gastritis: An immune-mediated destruction of parietal cells, linked to Vitamin B12 deficiency and pernicious anemia. Often associated with other autoimmune conditions.

Advanced Pathophysiological Dimensions

H. Pylori Mechanism

Urease production neutralizes gastric acid, allowing bacterial colonization and triggering cytokine-mediated inflammation. Virulence factors like CagA increase cancer risk.

COX-1 Inhibition

NSAIDs inhibit cyclooxygenase‑1, reducing the synthesis of protective prostaglandins that maintain mucosal blood flow and bicarbonate secretion.

Endoscopic Evaluation

Esophagogastroduodenoscopy (EGD) with biopsy remains the gold standard to visualize erythema, friability, and mucosal regenerative changes, and to obtain histology.

Correa Pathway & Gastric Neoplasia

The progression from superficial inflammation to gastric cancer follows the Correa cascade. It begins with non‑atrophic gastritis, advances to multifocal atrophic gastritis, then to intestinal metaplasia, and finally to dysplasia and adenocarcinoma. Molecular markers such as pepsinogen I/II ratios and serum gastrin levels are clinically useful to assess the degree of mucosal damage and cancer risk. Eradication of H. pylori at an early stage can halt or even reverse this progression.
