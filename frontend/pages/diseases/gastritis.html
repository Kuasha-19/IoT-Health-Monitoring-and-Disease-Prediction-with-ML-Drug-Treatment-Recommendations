Gastroenterology: Pathophysiology of Gastritis

Definition and Clinical Presentation

Gastritis is defined as the histological identification of inflammation within the gastric mucosa. It is not a single disease but a process occurring when the protective mucosal barrier is compromised, allowing hydrochloric acid and pepsin to cause autodigestion of the stomach lining. Clinical manifestations often include dyspepsia, postprandial fullness, and epigastric pain.

Etiology and Classification

The condition is broadly classified into Acute and Chronic forms. Acute gastritis often results from erosive agents like NSAIDs or alcohol. Chronic gastritis is frequently secondary to persistent infection by Helicobacter pylori, which induces a chronic inflammatory response, potentially leading to mucosal atrophy or intestinal metaplasia.

Types of Gastritis

  • Erosive Gastritis: Characterized by mucosal surface depletion and shallow ulcerations.
  • Non-erosive Gastritis: Primarily refers to histological changes induced by H. pylori infection.
  • Atrophic Gastritis: Chronic inflammation leading to the loss of glandular cells and intrinsic factor secretion.
  • Autoimmune Gastritis: An immune-mediated destruction of parietal cells, often linked to Vitamin B12 deficiency.

Clinical Dimensions and Advanced Pathophysiology

1. H. Pylori Colonization

Urease production by bacteria neutralizes gastric acid, allowing colonization of the antrum and triggering cytokine-mediated inflammation.

2. Prostaglandin Inhibition

Analysis of how non-selective NSAIDs inhibit COX-1 enzymes, reducing the synthesis of protective prostaglandins that maintain blood flow.

3. Endoscopic Evaluation

Diagnostic utilization of Esophagogastroduodenoscopy (EGD) to visualize erythema, friability, and mucosal regenerative changes.

4. Hypochlorhydria Risks

Long-term implications of reduced acid secretion on the microbiome and the potential for increased susceptibility to enteric infections.

5. Histopathological Staging

The Sydney System for classification, evaluating the density of neutrophils, mononuclear cells, and the presence of glandular atrophy.

Advanced Medical Analysis

The progression from superficial inflammation to gastric neoplasia follows the Correa Pathway. This involves a transition from non-atrophic gastritis to multifocal atrophic gastritis, followed by intestinal metaplasia (complete or incomplete). Molecular markers such as pepsinogen I/II ratios are clinically significant in assessing the degree of mucosal damage. Furthermore, the disruption of the cyto-protective bicarbonate layer and the failure of epithelial tight junctions represent the final common pathway for erosive mucosal injury.
